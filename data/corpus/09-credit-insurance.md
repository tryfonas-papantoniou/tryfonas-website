# Credit Insurance Claims

## What credit insurance does for us

Credit insurance protects a portion of the receivables book against customer default. It does not cover disputed amounts, political risk above the insured country cap, or transactions made in breach of policy terms.

The group uses a single whole-turnover policy covering the majority of the book, with named buyer limits on top for exposures above the policy's discretionary limit.

## Policy coverage

- **Insured percentage**: typically 90% of the invoice value after VAT and freight.
- **Minimum loss per claim**: 5,000 — below this the claim is not economic to file.
- **Waiting period**: 180 days from due date before a protracted default claim can be filed. Shorter for insolvency events — the claim can be filed once proof of insolvency is received.
- **Maximum liability**: sum insured per named buyer, plus a group aggregate cap.

Any deviation from the policy terms — for example, extending payment terms beyond what the insurer has approved — voids cover on that specific customer or transaction.

## What triggers a claim

Two routes:

1. **Insolvency claim** — the customer is formally insolvent. Triggers the moment we receive evidence (court filing, administrator appointment, notice of liquidation). Payment from the insurer typically within 30 days of filing a complete claim.
2. **Protracted default claim** — the customer has not paid and has not filed for insolvency. Triggers 180 days past the original due date, provided the collections cadence has been followed on time and is fully documented.

Both routes require the full collections history: every dunning letter sent, every phone call logged, every dispute case, every payment plan. The insurer will not pay a claim where the collections process was not run to the required standard.

## Notification of non-payment

The policy requires us to notify the insurer of any overdue above a threshold (typically 50,000) once it is 60 days past due. This is a notification only, not a claim — but missing the notification window can reduce the insurer's payout.

Notifications are tracked on a dedicated register. The credit analyst owns the register for their portfolio; the Credit Manager signs off weekly.

## Documentation checklist

When filing a claim, the credit analyst assembles:

- Signed sales contract or framework agreement.
- Purchase orders covering the claimed invoices.
- Invoices, delivery notes, and proof of delivery.
- Full collections history (reminders, calls, letters).
- Correspondence between our entity and the customer about the debt.
- Any payment plan documentation.
- Evidence of the insolvency event, if applicable.

Incomplete claims are rejected or adjusted downwards. The single most common reason for a reduced payout is a missing proof of delivery on a specific invoice.

## Limits set by the insurer

The insurer sets a per-buyer limit that caps the insured exposure. If our credit limit is higher than the insurer's limit, the uninsured portion is carried at our own risk and must be explicitly approved.

Annual renewal of the policy:

- New buyer limits applied for in October for the following calendar year.
- Renewal negotiated in November.
- New policy period starts 1 January.
- Mid-year limit changes can be requested but take four to six weeks.

## Interaction with write-off

A debt that has been claimed on insurance is not written off until the insurer has paid (or formally declined). If the insurer pays 90%, the remaining 10% is the uninsured portion and follows the normal write-off process.
